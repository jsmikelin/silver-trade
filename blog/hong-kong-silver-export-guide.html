Hong Kong Silver Export Guide 2026: LBMA Compliance, Customs Documentation and Shipping Logistics

📅 June 15, 2026📂 Export Guide⏱ 8 min read

Hong Kong has long served as a strategic hub for precious metals trading in Asia. Its free port status, robust financial infrastructure, proximity to mainland China's supply chain, and common law legal system make it an ideal base for international silver trading. For buyers in the United States and Japan, sourcing silver through Hong Kong offers distinct advantages — but navigating the export process requires a thorough understanding of LBMA compliance standards, customs documentation requirements, and logistics best practices. This guide provides a comprehensive overview for international silver buyers.

Why Hong Kong for Silver Trading?

Hong Kong's position as a global precious metals hub is built on several structural advantages:

  • Free port status: No customs duties or tariffs on precious metals entering or transiting through Hong Kong, making it a cost-effective transshipment point.
  • Strong banking sector: Major international banks with dedicated commodity trade finance desks facilitate smooth payment processing, letters of credit, and trade finance.
  • Proximity to supply: Hong Kong has direct access to Chinese silver refinement and manufacturing, which accounted for approximately 15% of global silver mine production in 2025.
  • International standards: Hong Kong-based traders commonly adhere to LBMA standards and provide SGS/CCIC third-party verification, meeting the quality requirements of the most demanding international buyers.
  • Language and expertise: Many Hong Kong trading firms employ English and Japanese-speaking account managers who understand the regulatory environments of their target markets.

LBMA Compliance and Quality Standards

The London Bullion Market Association (LBMA) Good Delivery standards are the de facto global benchmark for precious metals quality. While LBMA accreditation formally applies to refiners, reputable Hong Kong traders ensure their supply chain meets or exceeds these standards.

Key LBMA Requirements for Silver

  • Minimum purity: 99.9% (999 fineness) for Good Delivery silver bars. Most Hong Kong exporters supply 99.99% (4N) purity as standard.
  • Bar weight: Good Delivery bars range from 500 oz to 1,250 oz, though the most common specification traded internationally is 15kg (约 482oz) Standard Good Delivery Bars.
  • Serial numbering: Every bar must be individually serial-numbered for traceability.
  • Assay certificate: Each shipment must be accompanied by an independent assay certificate from an approved laboratory.
  • Manufacturer mark: The refiner's stamp must be clearly visible on each bar.

Important: While LBMA Good Delivery accreditation is held by refiners, not traders, Hong Kong Changjiang International Limited ensures all product sourced meets LBMA purity and documentation standards. Every shipment includes SGS or CCIC third-party assay certificates for full buyer confidence.

Shipping Logistics: Air Freight vs. Sea Freight

The choice between air and sea freight depends on shipment size, destination, and urgency.

Air Freight — Best for Small to Medium Shipments

Air freight is the preferred method for shipments under 500 kg, particularly for high-value silver bars and grains destined for the US and Japan. Transit times are typically 3–7 days from Hong Kong to major destinations like New York (JFK), Los Angeles (LAX), Tokyo (NRT), or Osaka (KIX). While rates are higher per kilogram than sea freight, the reduced transit time, lower insurance costs due to shorter exposure, and faster payment cycles often make air freight the more economical choice for smaller consignments.

Sea Freight — Best for Bulk Shipments

For large-volume shipments exceeding 1,000 kg, sea freight in standard 20-foot or 40-foot containers offers the most cost-effective solution. Transit times are 15–25 days to US West Coast ports and 10–18 days to major Japanese ports. Sea freight requires robust packaging and, for containerized cargo, tamper-evident seals. Full-container-load (FCL) shipments provide the best security and cost efficiency for bulk buyers.

Quality Assurance and Third-Party Verification

Reputable Hong Kong exporters engage independent inspection agencies to verify product quality before shipment. The two most commonly used agencies for precious metals are:

  • SGS: Global leader in inspection, verification, testing, and certification. SGS provides weight verification, purity testing via X-ray fluorescence (XRF) and fire assay methods, and packaging inspection.
  • CCIC (China Certification & Inspection Group): Widely recognized in Asian trade corridors, CCIC offers comparable testing and inspection services with deep expertise in the China–Hong Kong supply chain.

A standard pre-shipment inspection involves representative sampling, laboratory purity analysis, weight verification, and visual inspection for damage or contamination. The final assay certificate is a critical document for the buyer's customs clearance and internal quality records.

Payment Terms and Trade Finance

Standard payment terms for silver transactions between Hong Kong and international buyers include:

  • TT Wire Transfer: The most common method. A typical contract requires a 20% deposit upon contract signing, with the 80% balance due after price fixing and before shipment.
  • Letter of Credit (L/C): Preferred by first-time trading partners. An irrevocable, confirmed L/C from a prime bank provides security for both parties.
  • Price Fixing Mechanism: Many Hong Kong traders offer a flexible pricing window — buyers can fix the LME/SGE price at any point within 10 calendar days of contract signing, allowing them to time the market to their advantage.

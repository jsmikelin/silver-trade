US Silver Import Guide 2026: Regulations, Tariffs and Best Practices for Importers

📅 June 8, 2026📂 Import Guide⏱ 8 min read

The United States is one of the world's largest silver importing nations, consuming hundreds of millions of ounces annually for industrial fabrication, investment products, and jewelry manufacturing. For US buyers sourcing silver from Hong Kong — a premier global trading hub — navigating the import process correctly is essential to avoid delays, penalties, and unexpected costs. This comprehensive guide covers US import regulations, tariff classifications, customs clearance procedures, and best practices for smooth procurement.

HTS Classification and Duty Rates for Silver Imports

All goods imported into the United States must be classified under the Harmonized Tariff Schedule (HTS) of the United States. Silver is classified under Chapter 71 (Precious Stones and Metals). The relevant subheadings are:

HTS CodeDescriptionDuty Rate
7106.91.1000Unwrought silver (bars, ingots) — not over 99.9% pureFree (duty-free)
7106.91.5000Unwrought silver (bars, ingots) — over 99.9% pureFree (duty-free)
7106.92.1000Semi-manufactured silver (sheets, plates, wire) — not over 99.9%3.5% ad valorem
7106.92.5000Semi-manufactured silver (sheets, plates, wire) — over 99.9%3.5% ad valorem
7107.00.0000Base metal clad with silver2.5% ad valorem
7108.20.0000Gold (including gold plated with platinum)Free

Key Takeaway: Unwrought silver bullion — including 99.99% pure bars and grains — is duty-free under HTS 7106.91. This is a significant advantage for US importers. Semi-manufactured forms (sheet, wire, strip) face a 3.5% duty. Always verify correct classification with your customs broker.

Customs Clearance Process

Importing silver into the US requires filing with US Customs and Border Protection (CBP) through the Automated Commercial Environment (ACE). The Importer of Record (IOR) — typically the US buyer or their licensed customs broker — is responsible for accurate filing and payment of any applicable duties. The process involves the following steps:

  1. Pre-arrival filing: Entry documents must be filed within 15 days of the cargo's arrival at the US port of entry.
  2. Documentation submission: Commercial invoice, packing list, air waybill or bill of lading, and any applicable certificates (assay, origin).
  3. Bond requirement: A continuous customs bond (typically $50,000) or single-entry bond sufficient to cover the shipment value plus estimated duties.
  4. CBP review and inspection: Most bullion shipments clear without physical inspection, though CBP may hold shipments for documentation verification. Random examinations occur under the Automated Targeting System (ATS).
  5. Release and duty payment: Once CBP clears the entry, goods are released. Any duties owed are paid via the ACE system.

Key US Regulatory Agencies and Requirements

Beyond CBP, several other US regulatory agencies may have jurisdiction over precious metal imports:

Financial Crimes Enforcement Network (FinCEN)

While FinCEN's requirements primarily apply to dealers within the US, importers purchasing silver as an investment should be aware of Bank Secrecy Act (BSA) obligations. Commercial importers buying for industrial use generally are not subject to BSA requirements, but buying or selling silver as a financial investment may trigger reporting obligations for transactions exceeding $10,000.

Commodity Futures Trading Commission (CFTC)

The CFTC regulates commodity markets including silver futures and options. While CFTC registration is not required for simply importing physical silver, firms engaging in futures hedging or leveraged transactions should consult regulatory counsel.

Recent Trade Policy Considerations in 2026

US trade policy has seen significant changes in recent years. While silver bullion (HTS 7106.91) has historically been duty-free and has generally been exempt from Section 232 (steel and aluminum) and Section 301 (China trade) tariffs, importers should remain vigilant:

  • Country of origin matters: Silver refined or processed in Hong Kong may have a different origin status than silver sourced from mainland China. Hong Kong's separate WTO status and customs territory are generally recognized, which has historically protected Hong Kong-origin goods from Section 301 tariffs.
  • Documentation is critical: A properly issued Certificate of Origin is your best defense against tariff misclassification.
  • Monitor for changes: Trade policy evolves rapidly. Work with a customs broker who monitors CBP rulings and Federal Register notices for any changes affecting precious metal imports.

Best Practices for US Silver Importers

1. Vet Your Supplier Thoroughly

Work with established Hong Kong exporters who can provide verifiable trade references, sample assay certificates, and a clear chain of custody. Hong Kong Changjiang International Limited, for instance, maintains CCIC verification on every shipment and provides full documentation packages.

2. Use a Licensed Customs Broker

A customs broker experienced in precious metals imports ensures accurate HTS classification, proper entry filing, and timely clearance. They can also advise on duty drawback opportunities and free trade agreement benefits if applicable.

3. Understand the Price Fixing Mechanism

Many Hong Kong suppliers offer a flexible pricing window, allowing you to fix the final silver price within 10 days of contract signing. This gives you control over timing your purchase based on market conditions. The typical structure involves a 20% deposit, followed by price fixing, then payment of the 80% balance before shipment.

4. Insure Your Cargo

Precious metals shipments should always be insured at their full declared value. Marine cargo insurance, typically arranged by the exporter but confirmable by the buyer, provides protection against loss, theft, and damage during transit.

5. Maintain Documentation Records

Keep complete import records for at least five years. CBP may conduct post-entry audits, and having a complete file — contracts, invoices, packing lists, certificates, and customs entries — will facilitate any inquiries.

6. Choose the Right Incoterm

For first-time transactions, CIF (Cost, Insurance, and Freight) terms shift transport logistics to the supplier while giving you control at destination. For established relationships, FOB Hong Kong terms may offer better pricing if you have dedicated logistics arrangements.

US Destinations: Ports of Entry and Logistics

Silver shipped from Hong Kong typically enters the United States through several major ports of entry:

  • Los Angeles / Long Beach (California): The busiest US gateway for Asian imports. Excellent for shipments to West Coast, Midwest, and Southwestern markets.
  • New York / Newark (New Jersey): Primary East Coast gateway. Ideal for Northeastern and Mid-Atlantic buyers.
  • Chicago (Illinois): Major inland hub with Foreign Trade Zone (FTZ) status, allowing deferred duty payments.
  • Houston (Texas): Growing import hub for precious metals, particularly for Gulf Coast and Southern markets.

Air freight typically arrives at JFK (New York), LAX (Los Angeles), or ORD (Chicago). Sea freight enters through Long Beach, Newark, or Savannah depending on the carrier's service routes.
